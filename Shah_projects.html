Entrepreneurship Project

AP Computer Science Applications, April 2021 - May 2021

The goal of this project was to create a wildfire tracking dashboard that uses machine learning to analyzes footage from different camera feeds in real time in order to notify fire agencies of fire activity and help them respond promptly. I helped develop the idea and researched existing solutions to brainstorm our approach. After this, I developed the model by architecting a machine learning model that utilizes a convolutional neural network to classify images based on if they have a fire or not with Tensorflow. To train the model, I collected data and ran the training process until the model was 99% accurate on training data with no false negatives. Then, I exported the model for use on the client side. With this, I designed and developed the dashboard, creating our brand identity as well as the React-powered frontend. I connected this logic to use the model and progressed to a working prototype, complete with a map, activity feed, and live camera views. Virus Simulation Project

AP Computer Science Applications, August 2020

The goal of this project was to extend a virus simulation to incorporate new aspects of virus spread to bring it closer to reality. My team and I brainstormed different behaviors and factors which could affect the spread of the virus and result in different curves of infections or mortalities. These included accounting for immunity, self-isolation, wearing a mask, and a maximum hospital capacity. Beginning from a base project, we added these features incrementally and ensured each was working as we went. From the very beginning, we encountered problems with modularity and extending our existing code base as we attempted to represent the various different states an individual person could be in. In the end, we devised a sum type to represent the state of the health of a person and then used multiple flags to keep track of behaviors as they could be simultaneous. The next large step was to implement virus spread with a particle system, which we did by creating a new class to represent the virus and adding logic to handle spread and infection. After this, we accounted for self-isolation by stopping one's movement, wearing a mask by limiting particle spread, and maximum hospital capacity by increasing the mortality rate. In the end, I was comfortable working with Java and had gotten accustomed to its general patterns for abstraction through a hierarchy of classes as well as the actor pattern through the Greenfoot API. Solar-Hydrogen Alternative Energy Vehicle

Principles of Engineering, October 2019 - November 2019

The goal of this project was to create a vehicle that would be powered by alternative energy sources, namely solar panels and hydrogen fuel cells, to see which combination and design would work the best for a client. In the end, we needed a strong and fast vehicle that allowed different configurations of energy sources and circuit types. My team and I began by brainstorming potential designs. I proposed a design that was in the shape of a rectangular prism with an open bottom to hold the wheel and axle machine, allowing it to either carry a load underneath or have a clear path for air to flow through. On top, there was space for the motor, breadboard, and energy sources. After we got the design finalized, we split up the work, and I worked on making a functioning series and parallel circuit that worked with a switch mounted on the breadboard. However, when connecting the energy sources to the main chassis, we ran into a problem: hydrogen cells and solar panels would not mount interchangeably. As a result, the team and I brainstormed a solution: small poles that held the hydrogen cells securely, but also had a place for screws on top for solar panels. By the end I was able to comfortably design series and parallel circuits and explain the different mechanisms that made up our vehicle. In addition, I learned about energy and power, specifically the tradeoff and balance between force and distance. In conclusion, we followed the engineering design process to iterate on our design and separate the work to build it within the deadline, and I learned a lot about configurable designs and different aspects of engineering in the process. Compound Machine Project

Principles of Engineering, August 2019 - September 2019

The goal of this project was to create a compound machine that would be useful to an alien species. My team and I brainstormed a problem: it can be hard to climb up a slide on a playground after riding it. As a result, we designed a compound machine that works by exposing a wheel that a user can spin. This wheel drives an axle, which drives a simple gear train, which spins a pulley, pulling the rider of the slide back up the inclined plane. My role in the project involved designing the original idea and implementing it using VEX parts. Specifically, I created the original sketches, laying out how different simple machines could interact. Then, we split up into pairs within the team that would each implement two simple machines. After working with a teammate to create the gear train and the wheel and axle, I worked with the rest of the team to connect the parts. However, we ran into an obstacle when attaching the “pulley” — we realized that it was resembling a wheel more than a pulley, as it simply winded up a string. This meant that we did not meet the constraint of having four types of simple machines. I took the lead and suggested moving the wheel below the base of the gear train so that it would change the direction of force and act as a true pulley. Throughout the project, I was able to delve deeper into mechanical engineering, an area I have an interest in but have not yet explored thoroughly; I learned about mechanical advantage and the design process behind compound machines. In the end, my teammates and I worked well together by distributing work evenly, and I was able to demonstrate leadership and quick-thinking when we came across an unexpected challenge. Image Manipulation Project

Computer Science Principles, February 2019 - March 2019

The goal of this project was to make software that manipulates input images in some way that is beneficial to a developer client looking for different ways to transform images. As a result, my partner and I for this project brainstormed “The Transformer”, a general tool that comes with predefined transformations on images and is easily extensible to allow for more. We came up with a list of potential transformations, including converting images to the style of Vincent Van Gogh, converting images to images made up of Minecraft blocks, and making pixel art from images. My role in the project was to develop the transform that turns landscape images into paintings in the style of Vincent Van Gogh. To get started, I looked into artificial intelligence techniques and learned a lot about Generative Adversarial Networks. I created my own implementation of one in Python using the Numpy, Keras, and Tensorflow libraries. One challenge that I faced was implementing the model and optimizing hyperparameters for it. I had a lot of trouble when dimensions didn’t work out because of the 5 dimensional matrices that I was working with, but eventually got it after researching convolutional neural networks and understanding how they work. Teamwork was required in this project, as the project wouldn’t be called the Transformer without the Minecraft transform created by my partner. I took the lead in the project and created most of the documentation, and, in addition, I distributed roles and tasks between me and my partner to get our modules connected and working together in the end.
